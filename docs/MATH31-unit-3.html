Series and sequences

What is a series

To evaluate series, first find the partial sum:

∑n=1∞nSn=1+2+3+ ... +n

Find the formula for Sn

Sn=n(n+1)2

Take the limit as n→∞

limn→∞n(n+1)2=∞

Telescoping

These series look like two repeating fractions that end up canceling everything except something from the first term and something from the last. For example:

∑n=1∞(12n+3−12n+1)

First find the partial sum Sn

Sn=(15−13)+(17−15)+(19−17)+ ...+ (12n+1−12n−1)+(12n+3−12n+1)

Almost all of these fractions will cancel if you see the patern. The only 2 left are:

Sn=−13+12n+3

Take the limit of this partial sum Sn

limn→∞[−13+12n+3]=−13

Geometric

Geometric series take the form of:

∑n=1∞arn−1

The series will convert of |r|<1, otherwise it will diverge.

If the sum does convert, the sum is:

∑n=1∞arn−1=a1−r

Shortcut

If the first power of the sequence is 0 then the first term is a. a stands for the first term in your series.

For example:

∑n=1∞(23)n=∑n=1∞(23)(23)n−1=231−23

Another example:

∑n=2∞en3n+1=∑n=2∞en3∙3n=∑n=2∞13(e3)n

The mistake most people make here is thinking that a=13. This isn't the case because plugging in n=2 doesn't make the first exponent 0. So split off more e3's to make it in the right form:

=∑n=2∞13(33)2(e3)n−2=∑n=2∞e227(e3)n−2

Now since the first term makes the exponent go to 0. You can tell what a and r are now. So:

=e2271−e23

So the shortcut here is that you can start with

=∑n=2∞13(e3)n

and simply plug in 2 for n (the starting point). Since we know that the first term is a you can jump to the answer:

=e2271−e23

Harmonic

Harmonc series are defined as:

∑n=1∞1n=11+12+13+ ... +1n

Harmonic series are divergent. If a sequence {an} is convergent, any subsequence of {an} must also be convergent. To show that a sequence {an} diverges, it is enough to show that a subsequence diverges.

Note: If a series converges, then

∑n=1∞anlimn→∞an=0

So to show a series diverges, it's enough to show:

limn→∞an≠0

If the limit doesn't equal 0, or DNE, the series {an} diverges.

Remember! The limit equaling 0 does NOT necessarily mean convergence!

Example

∑n=1∞2n2−13n2−1limn→∞2n2−13n2−1≠0

Diverges because the limit equals 0!

Properties of convergent series

  1. You can always pull a constant out in front of the series

∑n=1∞Can=C∑n=1∞an

  1. You can split sequences on sums or differences

∑n=1∞(an±bn)=∑n=1∞an±∑n=1∞bn

Example

∑n=1∞(2n−5n3n)=∑n=1∞(2n3n−5n3n)=∑n=1∞2n3n−∑n=1∞5n3n

If we split this sequence into parts, each part much be convergent for the entire sequence to be convergent! If any single part is divergent then the entire thing is divergent.

=∑n=1∞(23)n−∑n=1∞(53)n

In this form we can evaluate them as geometric series. Automatically we know this is divergent because the rightmost fraction's r is greater than 1. Since a subsequence of the original diverges, the original does too.

Integral test

For f(n)=an, if f(n) is continous, positive, and decreasing, then we can use the integral to show convergence/divergence of our series.

So:

∑n=1∞ and ∫∞1f(x) dx

will have the same result (either converge or diverge).

  • This can tell you convergence/divergence, but does not necessarily give the sum of the series.
  • Convergence is not affected by the addition or subtraction of a finite number of terms from our series.

We can judge the convergence of ∑∞n=1an with:

∑n=1∞an or ∫∞1f(x) dx

But to do the integral test, we can start the integral at N, the sum of our integral will not be the sum of the series, but we can at least tell if it converges/diverges.

∫∞Nf(x) dx

Example

∑n=1∞1n2+1

First thing first, you should check the divergence by taking the limit. The limit here equals 0, so it fails the divergence test. (It may be divergent some other way, but we don't know. It may be convergent, but we don't know). It's not telescoping, its not factorable, so lets try the integral test.

f(x)=1x2+1

This should act as an upper bound for our sequence provided its always positive, continuous, and decreasing on it's interval [1,∞). If it meets these requirements then we can do the integral test:

∫∞11x2+1dx

This is an improper integral:

limb→∞∫b11x2+1dx=limb→∞[tan−1x]b1=limb→∞[tan−1b−tan−11]=π2−π4=π4

Since we got a number, that shows that the series must converge! Our answer from the integral is not necessarily the sum of the series!

Example

∑n=1∞32n−1

Try the divergence test first. The limit is 0 so it doesn't automatically diverge.

The integral test:

f(x)=32x−1

It isn't always positive, but it is on our interval [1,∞). It is continuous on our interval and it is also decreasing, so lets try the integral test.

∫∞132x−1=limb→∞∫b132x−1=limb→∞32∫1udu=limb→∞32ln [2x−1]b1=32limb→∞[ln(2b−1)−ln(2−1)]=∞u=2x−1du=2dx

Since the limit evaluates to ∞ the integral diverges. So the series also diverges.

Example

∑n=1∞lnnnf(x)=lnxx

The function is positive and continuous. To show decreasing show that f′(x)<0:

f′(x)1e=1−lnxx2≤lnx≤x

Choose a interval where f(x) will be decreasing. f(x) will certainly be decreasing on the interval [3,∞).

So:

∫∞3lnxx=limb→∞∫b3lnxxdx=limb→∞∫b3u du=limb→∞[12(lnx)2]b3=limb→∞[12(lnb)2−12(ln3)2]=∞u=lnxdu=1xdx

Therefore, the integral diverges. So the series also diverges.

P series

∑n=1∞1nP

When P<1 the series will diverge.

limn→∞1nP

When P>1 the series will converge (can be shown with the integral test).

Examples

  1. ∑n=1∞1n2

    Here we can see that P=2 (greater than 1), so the series must converge.

  2. ∑n=1∞1n‾√3

    Here we can see that P=13 (less than 1), so the series diverges.

  3. ∑n=1∞n−π=∑n=1∞1nπ

    Since P=π (greater than 1) the series must converge.

More integral test problems

1

∑n=1∞e1nn2

First do the divergence test, the limit is 0 so it doesn't immediately diverge. Its not a geometric series. Its also not a P-series. Let's try the integral test.

f(x)=e1nn2

The function must be positive, continuous, and decreasing on the interval. The function is positive. Its only discontinuous at 0 and thats not in our interval. To show decreasing make sure f′(x)<0 (alternatively you could show that an−1<an):

f′(x)=−e1x−2xe1xx4=−e1x(1+2x)x4

This is negative on our interval [1,∞).

So lets do the integral test:

∫∞1e1xx2dx=limb→∞∫b1e1xx2dx=−limb→∞∫b1eudu=−limb→∞[e1x]b1=−limb→∞[e1b−e1]=−[1−e]=e−1udu=1x=−1x2dx

Therefore since the integral converges, the series must converge also.
